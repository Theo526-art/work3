Venus: Earth’s Fiery Twin with a Hidden Past

Introduction

Venus, often called Earth's twin, is a planet of paradoxes. Similar in size and composition to our own world, it hides beneath thick clouds a landscape of volcanic plains, towering mountains, and surface temperatures hot enough to melt lead. Despite its beauty when seen from Earth — shining brightly as the "Morning Star" or "Evening Star" — Venus is the most inhospitable planet in the solar system.

Fast Facts about Venus

  • Distance from Sun: ~108.2 million km (67.2 million miles)
  • Length of a Year: 225 Earth days
  • Diameter: 12,104 km (7,521 miles)
  • Surface Temperature: ~465°C (869°F)
  • Atmosphere: Thick, mostly CO₂ with clouds of sulfuric acid

A Spinning Oddity

Venus spins backward (retrograde rotation), meaning the Sun rises in the west and sets in the east — the opposite of Earth. Even more strange: its day is longer than its year. This slow and reversed spin is a mystery, possibly the result of a massive ancient collision or gravitational influences from the Sun and other planets.

A Hellish Greenhouse World

Venus is the victim of a runaway greenhouse effect. Its thick atmosphere traps solar radiation, causing surface temperatures to soar higher than on Mercury, even though it’s farther from the Sun. The atmosphere is 90 times denser than Earth’s and made up of 96% carbon dioxide, with clouds of sulfuric acid that reflect sunlight, giving Venus its bright appearance. The extreme heat and pressure make the surface as harsh as the deep ocean floor — crushing and scorching anything that lands there.

Surface Features and Volcanoes

Though hidden beneath permanent clouds, radar mapping has revealed a complex surface:

  • Volcanoes: Venus has more volcanoes than any other planet — possibly over 1,600 major ones. Some scientists believe a few may still be active.
  • Mountains: The tallest is Maxwell Montes, rising 11 km (7 miles), higher than Everest.
  • Tesserae: These are unique, tile-like ridged terrains, suggesting complex tectonic forces.

    • At about 50–60 km above the surface, the pressure and temperature are more Earth-like — a zone scientists refer to when discussing future aerial habitats or probes.

Missions to Venus

Venus has been visited more than 40 times by spacecraft, yet it remains largely unexplored:

  • Program (USSR): The only probes to land and transmit data from Venus’s surface. Venera 13 (1982) survived for 127 minutes.
  • (NASA, 1989–1994): Mapped 98% of Venus using radar.
  • Akatsuki (JAXA, 2015–present): Currently studying Venus’s atmosphere and weather from orbit.

Conclusion

Venus may appear serene in the sky, but it's a world of scorching heat, crushing pressure, and toxic clouds. Still, its similarities to Earth make it a key to understanding planetary evolution, atmospheric science, and climate change. As new missions aim to pierce its veil, Venus is poised to share its secrets — and perhaps even challenge what we think we know about life beyond Earth.
